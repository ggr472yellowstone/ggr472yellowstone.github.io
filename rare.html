Rare Species

Pinyon Jay - Gymnorhinus cyanocephalus

The Pinyon Jay nests in trees located in open woodland areas. These birds forage for insects, seeds, berries, and very small animals on the ground. They fly in large loud groups and are members of permanent flocks that can contain around 500 birds. The Pinyon Jays estimated breeding population is 690,000 due to the degradation of their Pinyon-juniper forest habitat. So, if you see a small gray-blue bird with a brighter blue head, consider yourself a lucky birder!

Boreal Owl - Aegolius funereus

The Boreal Owl is a breeding species in Yellowstone National Park named for their time spent in Boreal forests. These owls hunt during the nighttime and eat small mammals. The female Boreal Owl is much larger and heavier than the male. Mated pairs meet only for courting and feeding. These owls are found across North America and Eurasia, being partly migratory during seasons of prey scarcity like the winter, flying south.
Find more information on the Boreal Owl here!

Black-backed Woodpecker - Picoides arcticus

The Black-Backed Woodpecker is a breeding species in Yellowstone National Park. These birds seek out burned forests that have recently been affected by a forest fire in order to live off of the bounty of insects this brings. Like other woodpecker species, the Black-backed Woodpecker excavates its nest itself out of wood, creating a nest cavity.
Find more information on the Black-backed Woodpecker here!

House Finch - Haemorhous mexicanus

The House Finch is a small and strictly vegetarian bird, sticking to a diet of berries and seeds. These small birds can also drink up to 40% of their weight in water, so you are likely to find them attracted to water sources. The House Finch is native to the Western United States, but after being smuggled from California to New York in the 40’s became more prevalent in the East and all across North America.
Find more information on the House Finch here!

White-winged Crossbill - Loxia leucoptera

The White-winged Crossbill is native to Canada and few western US states.These are small 6-inch long birds that feast on conifer seeds. They reside in spruce and winter forests throughout the year. This species is far more prevalent in the north and tends to come down to the US during winter, but is not well-monitored.
Find more information on the White-winged Crossbill here!

American Goldfinch - Spinus tristis

The American Goldfinch is named for its vibrant yellow body; it is also adorned with black and white wings. Although, this vibrant yellow is seen only on breeding males during the spring and summer, with females having a duller yellow, and both becoming a dull brown in the winter. They are energetic finches that are small enough to land on weeds and small branches. In fact, their habitat consists of weedy fields and plains!
Find more information on the American Goldfinch here!

Virginia Rail - Rallus limicola

The Virginia Rail is a mysterious character that sneaks through freshwater marches throughout North America. These birds eat aquatic invertebrates like small fish, frogs, snails, and insects. The Virginia Rail builds its nest in wetland vegetation that can be floating on the water’s surface.
